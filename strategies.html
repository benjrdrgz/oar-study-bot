1. OAR Test Overview
Know what you're walking into
▼

The Officer Aptitude Rating (OAR) is the first 3 sections of the ASTB-E (Aviation Standard Test Battery). It measures your potential as a Navy/Marine Corps officer candidate.

Key Facts
Format: Computer Adaptive Test (CAT)
Score range: 20–80 (average 40–60)
Attempts: 3 lifetime (Navy uses your MOST RECENT score)
No calculator allowed. You get scratch paper and a pencil.

2. How Adaptive Testing Works
This is the single most important thing to understand
▼
CRITICAL — Read This Carefully
The OAR is a Computer Adaptive Test. The test adjusts difficulty based on your answers. Understanding this changes everything about your strategy.

How CAT Works

  • Correct answer → next question is harder → your score ceiling goes UP
  • Wrong answer → next question is easier → your score ceiling goes DOWN
  • You cannot skip or go back to previous questions
  • Running out of time counts as wrong answers — this is a huge penalty
  • The first 5–7 questions are critical — they set your difficulty floor
How CAT Scoring Works
Key Insight
When questions get harder, that means you're doing well. Don't panic. Getting a hard question wrong still scores higher than getting an easy question right.

3. Section-by-Section Strategy
Specific tactics for each part of the OAR
▼

MATH Math Skills Strategy

  • Take extra time on the first 5 questions. These set your difficulty floor in CAT. Getting them right is crucial.
  • 2-minute max per question. If you're stuck after 2 minutes, make your best educated guess and move on.
  • Estimate before calculating. Often you can eliminate 2–3 answers just by ballparking.
  • Check your units. Minutes vs hours, feet vs inches. Unit mismatches are the most common careless error.
  • No calculator. Practice mental math daily. Know your times tables and common fractions cold.
Common Math Traps
• PEMDAS violations (mult/div before add/sub!)
• Markup then discount ≠ original price (30% markup then 30% off ≠ original)
• Forgetting to flip the inequality sign when multiplying/dividing by a negative
• Mixing up radius and diameter in circle problems

READING Reading Comprehension Strategy

The Golden Rule
If the passage didn't explicitly say it → it's WRONG. Even if it's true in real life. The only correct answer is what the passage states or directly implies.
  • This is the easiest section — bank your points here. Most people score highest on RC.
  • Read the first and last sentences of each paragraph for the main idea. Skim the middle for details.
  • Use keywords from the question to locate the relevant part of the passage. Don't re-read the whole thing.
  • Steady pace: ~90 seconds per question. Don't rush, don't linger.

Elimination Strategy

  • Too extreme — words like "always," "never," "all," "none" are usually wrong
  • True but not stated — might be factually true but not in the passage = wrong
  • Opposite — says the reverse of what the passage says
  • Too narrow — focuses on one detail when the question asks about the main idea

Author's purpose clues: "should" / "must" = persuade. Statistics / data = inform. Story / anecdote = entertain or illustrate.

MECHANICAL Mechanical Comprehension Strategy

30 Seconds Per Question
This is the fastest-paced section. You either know it or you don't. Spend 30 seconds max, then make your best guess and move on. Never let time run out.
  • Mostly conceptual, not heavy math. You rarely need to crunch numbers — just understand the concept.
  • Focus your study on: levers, gears, pulleys, Ohm's law, and pressure. These make up the bulk of questions.
  • Draw quick diagrams on your scratch paper for gear direction and lever problems.
Common Mechanical Traps
• Newton's 3rd law pairs act on different objects
• Kinetic energy: velocity is squared (double speed = 4x energy)
• Gear direction alternates (adjacent = opposite)
• Series circuits: one break kills everything. Parallel: branches are independent.

4. Score Optimization
Where to focus for maximum point gain
▼

Priority Stack

Not all sections are created equal. Here's where to focus your study time for maximum ROI:

1
Reading Comprehension
Easiest section. Bank your points here. Most people can ace this with the right technique.
2
Math Skills
Biggest swing potential. A few days of focused formula review can move your score 5–10 points.
3
Mechanical Comprehension
Hardest to improve, but quick study of key concepts helps the most. Don't ignore it.
CAT Scoring Insight
In adaptive testing, accuracy on harder questions matters MORE than raw count. Getting 20 questions right at a hard difficulty level scores higher than getting 25 right at an easy level.

Guessing Strategy

  • Educated guess (eliminate 1–2 answers) > wild guess > no answer
  • Never leave a question blank. Unanswered = wrong, and in CAT, running out of time is penalized heavily.
  • If you can eliminate even one answer choice, your odds jump from 25% to 33%.

5. Time Management
Pace yourself to maximize every minute
▼

Section Timer Guide

Math Skills (40 min, ~30 questions) ~80 sec/question
Start (slow) Q5 — speed up Q20 — check pace Q25 — finish strong
Reading (30 min, ~20 questions) ~90 sec/question
Steady pace Q10 — halfway check Q15 — stay steady
Mechanical (15 min, ~30 questions) ~30 sec/question
FAST from the start Know it or guess Never stall

When to Guess vs When to Grind

  • Math: If you don't see the path within 30 seconds, try plugging in answer choices. If still stuck at 2 min, guess.
  • Reading: If the answer isn't obvious after one re-read of the relevant paragraph, eliminate and guess.
  • Mechanical: 30 seconds. Period. If you don't recognize the concept, pick the most reasonable answer and move.
Never Leave Questions Unanswered
In CAT, unanswered questions at the end are scored as wrong at an easy difficulty. This tanks your score far more than a wrong guess on a hard question. Finish every section even if you have to rapid-guess the last few.

6. Common Mistakes That Cost Points
Avoid these and you're already ahead of most test-takers
▼
  1. Running out of time on Mechanical. 15 minutes for 30 questions is brutal. You must move fast. Practice speed.
  2. Spending too long on hard math questions. The 2-minute rule exists for a reason. One hard question isn't worth missing 3 easy ones at the end.
  3. Not reading RC questions carefully. Read what they're ASKING. "Main idea" vs "detail" vs "inference" require different approaches.
  4. Using outside knowledge on RC. The answer must come from the passage. Your real-world knowledge is irrelevant here.
  5. Mixing up gear/pulley direction rules. Adjacent gears go opposite. Odd chain = same direction as first gear. Practice until it's automatic.
  6. Forgetting there's no calculator. If you haven't practiced mental math, you'll waste time on simple arithmetic under pressure.
  7. Panicking when questions get harder. Harder questions = you're doing WELL. This is exactly what you want in CAT. Stay calm.
  8. Not using scratch paper. Drawing diagrams, writing out equations, organizing your work — scratch paper is your biggest advantage.
  9. Retaking without more study. Your most recent score counts. Don't retake hoping for luck. Study more, then retake with confidence.

8. Mental Game
Your mindset is worth 5–10 points
▼
The #1 Mindset Shift
Harder questions = you're doing well. In CAT, difficulty going up means the computer thinks you're smart. Embrace it. A wrong answer on a hard question barely hurts you. A wrong answer on an easy question kills your score.

During the Test

  • Stay calm, breathe between sections. Take 3 deep breaths before starting each new section. Reset your mental state.
  • Don't dwell on questions you got wrong. You can't go back. The moment you submit, it's gone. Focus forward.
  • Confidence builds momentum in CAT. When you feel good, you think more clearly, you answer correctly, questions get harder (good!), and your score climbs. It's a positive spiral.
  • If you feel yourself spiraling, pause for 5 seconds. Close your eyes. Take one deep breath. Then read the next question fresh.

Visualization Technique

The night before and morning of the test, spend 2 minutes visualizing:

  1. You walk into the testing center calm and prepared
  2. You sit down, dump your formulas, and feel confident
  3. You work through each section at a steady pace
  4. When questions get hard, you smile — you know that means you're scoring high
  5. You finish the test, check your score, and see the number you wanted
This Actually Works
Sports psychologists use visualization with elite athletes. Studies show mental rehearsal activates the same neural pathways as physical practice. 2 minutes of visualization costs you nothing and can lower your test anxiety significantly.

9. Scratch Paper Strategy
Your scratch paper is your biggest weapon
▼

Formula Dump Protocol

  • Write formulas IMMEDIATELY before the first question. Don't wait.
  • Organize by section: one area for math formulas, one for mechanical concepts.
  • Write neatly but fast. You need to be able to read it under pressure.
  • Label variables in word problems. D = ?, R = ?, T = ?
  • Draw diagrams for mechanical questions. Levers, gears, pulleys — always draw first.
