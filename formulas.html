Arithmetic
5 formulas ▼
PEMDAS (Order of Operations)
Parentheses → Exponents → Mult/Div (L→R) → Add/Sub (L→R)
Multiplication and division are equal priority (left to right), same for addition and subtraction.
Example: 8 + 2 × 5 = 8 + 10 = 18 (NOT 50)
Please Excuse My Dear Aunt Sally
Fraction Operations
Add: (ad + bc) / bd   |   Mult: ac / bd   |   Div: flip & multiply
To add fractions, cross multiply and add. To divide, flip the second fraction and multiply.
Example: 1/3 + 1/4 = (4+3)/12 = 7/12
KCF for division: Keep, Change, Flip
Percent
Percent = (Part / Whole) × 100
Find what percentage a part is of the whole.
Example: 15 out of 60 = (15/60) × 100 = 25%
Percent Change
% Change = (New − Old) / Old × 100
Always divide by the ORIGINAL value. Positive = increase, negative = decrease.
Example: Price goes from $80 to $100: (100-80)/80 × 100 = 25% increase
New minus Old, over Old. Always Old on the bottom.
Cross Multiplication
a/b = c/d   →   ad = bc
Set up proportions and cross multiply to solve for unknowns.
Example: 3/x = 9/12 → 36 = 9x → x = 4

Algebra
3 formulas ▼
Exponent Rules
xa·xb = xa+b  |  xa/xb = xa−b  |  (xa)b = xab
x0 = 1  |  x−n = 1/xn
Multiply bases = add exponents. Divide bases = subtract exponents. Power of a power = multiply exponents.
Example: 23 × 24 = 27 = 128
Same base? Add when multiplying, subtract when dividing.
Quadratic Formula
x = (−b ± √(b² − 4ac)) / 2a
Solves ax² + bx + c = 0. The discriminant (b² − 4ac) tells you: positive = 2 solutions, zero = 1, negative = none.
Example: x² − 5x + 6 = 0 → x = (5 ± √(25−24))/2 = (5±1)/2 → x = 3 or 2
Negative boy couldn't decide (plus or minus) to go to the radical party. The boy was too square, he missed out on four awesome chicks. The party was over at 2am.
Inequalities
When you multiply or divide by a negative → FLIP the sign
The direction of the inequality reverses when multiplying or dividing both sides by a negative number.
Example: −2x > 6 → x < −3 (sign flipped!)
Negative flip! If you multiply/divide by negative, the sign does a backflip.

Geometry
6 formulas ▼
Pythagorean Theorem
a² + b² = c²
For right triangles only. c is always the hypotenuse (longest side, opposite the right angle).
Common triples: 3-4-5, 5-12-13, 8-15-17 and their multiples (6-8-10, 10-24-26)
Memorize 3-4-5 and 5-12-13 — they show up constantly on the OAR.
Special Right Triangles
45-45-90: x, x, x√2    |    30-60-90: x, x√3, 2x
45-45-90: two equal legs, hypotenuse = leg × √2. 30-60-90: short leg, long leg = short × √3, hypotenuse = 2 × short.
Example: 30-60-90 with short leg 5: sides are 5, 5√3, 10
Area Formulas
Rectangle: L×W  |  Triangle: ½bh  |  Circle: πr²  |  Trapezoid: ½(b₁+b₂)h
Remember: area is always in square units. Circle uses radius, not diameter.
Example: Circle with r=5: A = π(25) ≈ 78.54
Volume Formulas
Cylinder: πr²h  |  Sphere: ⅔πr³  |  Cone: ⅓πr²h
Cone = 1/3 of cylinder. Sphere = 4/3 πr cubed.
Example: Cylinder r=3, h=10: V = π(9)(10) = 90π ≈ 282.7
A cone is 1/3 of a cylinder — literally chop a cylinder into 3 cones.
Circumference
C = 2πr = πd
Circumference is the perimeter of a circle. Diameter = 2 × radius.
Example: r = 7: C = 2π(7) = 14π ≈ 43.98
Coordinate Geometry
Slope: (y₂−y₁)/(x₂−x₁)  |  Distance: √(Δx²+Δy²)  |  y = mx + b
Slope = rise over run. Distance formula is just Pythagorean theorem on a coordinate plane.
Example: Points (1,2) and (4,6): slope = (6−2)/(4−1) = 4/3
Rise over run: how much you go UP divided by how much you go RIGHT.

Word Problems
5 formulas ▼
Distance = Rate × Time
D = R × T
Also: R = D/T and T = D/R. Watch units: if rate is mph, time must be in hours!
Example: 60 mph for 2.5 hours = 150 miles
D-R-T triangle: cover what you need, the rest is the formula (just like VIR).
Combined Work Rate
1/A + 1/B = 1/T
A and B are individual completion times. T is time working together. Add the rates, not the times!
Example: Worker A: 6 hrs, Worker B: 3 hrs → 1/6 + 1/3 = 1/2 → Together = 2 hours
Add RATES (1/time), never add times directly.
Simple Interest
I = P × r × t
P = principal (initial amount), r = rate (as decimal), t = time (usually years).
Example: $1000 at 5% for 3 years: I = 1000 × 0.05 × 3 = $150
Probability
P = favorable outcomes / total outcomes
Always between 0 and 1 (or 0% to 100%). P(not A) = 1 − P(A).
Example: Drawing a heart from a deck: 13/52 = 1/4 = 25%
Average (Mean)
Average = Sum of all values / Number of values
Can rearrange: Sum = Average × Count. Useful for "what score do I need" problems.
Example: Scores: 85, 90, 92, 88 → Average = 355/4 = 88.75
To find a missing value: Total needed = target avg × count, then subtract known values.

Forces & Motion
3 formulas ▼
Newton's 2nd Law
F = m × a
Force (Newtons) = mass (kg) × acceleration (m/s²). More mass or more acceleration = more force.
Example: 10 kg object, a = 3 m/s²: F = 30 N
Weight
W = m × g   (g = 9.8 m/s²)
Weight is a force. Mass stays the same everywhere; weight changes with gravity.
Example: 70 kg person: W = 70 × 9.8 = 686 N
Free Fall
v = gt   |   d = ½gt²
Objects in free fall accelerate at g = 9.8 m/s² (no air resistance). Velocity and distance increase with time.
Example: After 3 seconds: v = 9.8(3) = 29.4 m/s, d = ½(9.8)(9) = 44.1 m

Levers & Torque
4 formulas ▼
Lever Principle
F₁ × D₁ = F₂ × D₂
Force times distance on one side equals force times distance on the other. This is how seesaws work.
Example: 50 lb at 4 ft = ? at 2 ft → 200 = 2x → x = 100 lb
Mechanical Advantage (Lever)
MA = Effort Arm / Load Arm
MA > 1 means you use less force. Longer effort arm = more advantage.
Example: Effort arm 8 ft, load arm 2 ft: MA = 4 (you lift 4x the weight)
Lever Classes
1st: Fulcrum in middle  |  2nd: Load in middle  |  3rd: Effort in middle
1st class (seesaw): F-L-E or E-L-F. 2nd class (wheelbarrow): F-L-E. 3rd class (fishing rod): L-E-F.
1st Class (Seesaw)
2nd Class (Wheelbarrow)
3rd Class (Fishing Rod)
FLE = 1-2-3. 1st: Fulcrum middle. 2nd: Load middle. 3rd: Effort middle.
Torque
τ = F × d
Torque is rotational force. d is the perpendicular distance from the pivot to the force line. Units: N·m.
Example: 20 N force at 0.5 m from pivot: τ = 10 N·m

Gears
4 formulas ▼
Gear Ratio
T₁ × RPM₁ = T₂ × RPM₂
Teeth × RPM is constant across meshed gears. More teeth = slower speed.
Example: Gear A: 20 teeth at 100 RPM, Gear B: 40 teeth → RPM = 2000/40 = 50 RPM
Gear Direction Rules
Adjacent gears = opposite direction
Odd # in chain = same direction  |  Even # = opposite
Two meshed gears always spin opposite. Count gears from driver: odd count = same, even = opposite.
2 Gears (Opposite)
3 Gears (Same as 1st)
Just alternate! CW, CCW, CW, CCW... Odd gear in chain = same as first gear.
Gear Speed Rule
Smaller gear = faster   |   Larger gear = slower
Fewer teeth = fewer contact points per revolution = spins faster. A small gear driving a large gear slows things down (but increases torque).
Example: A 10-tooth gear drives a 30-tooth gear. The large gear spins at 1/3 the speed.
Idler Gear
Idler gear changes direction but NOT speed ratio
A gear inserted between driver and driven only reverses direction. The speed ratio depends only on first and last gear.

Pulleys
4 formulas ▼
Fixed Pulley
MA = 1   (direction change only)
A fixed pulley doesn't reduce force needed. It just lets you pull down instead of up.
Movable Pulley
MA = 2
A movable pulley cuts the force needed in half, but you pull twice the distance.
Block & Tackle
MA = # of supporting ropes
Count the number of rope segments supporting the load (not the pull rope). That's your MA.
Example: 4 ropes supporting 200 lb load: Force needed = 200/4 = 50 lb
Fixed (MA=1)
Movable (MA=2)
Combined (MA=4)
Pulley Force Formula
Force = Weight / MA
Trade-off: less force = more rope to pull. You never get something for nothing (conservation of energy).
Example: 300 lb load, MA=3: Pull force = 100 lb (but pull 3x the distance)

Energy & Work
6 formulas ▼
Work
W = F × d
Work (Joules) = Force (N) × distance (m) in the direction of the force. No movement = no work!
Example: Push 50 N for 3 m: W = 150 J
Kinetic Energy
KE = ½mv²
Energy of motion. Velocity is SQUARED, so double the speed = 4x the energy. This is a common trap!
Example: 2 kg at 3 m/s: KE = ½(2)(9) = 9 J
v is SQUARED! If speed doubles, KE quadruples. This is the #1 KE trap on the OAR.
Potential Energy
PE = mgh
Energy stored by position. Higher up = more PE. At ground level, PE = 0 (if ground is reference).
Example: 5 kg at 10 m: PE = 5 × 9.8 × 10 = 490 J
Power
P = W / t   (Watts)
Power is how FAST you do work. Same work in less time = more power.
Example: 500 J in 10 seconds: P = 50 W
Inclined Plane
MA = Length / Height
A ramp trades distance for force. Longer ramp = less force needed to reach the same height.
Example: Ramp 12 ft long, 3 ft high: MA = 4
Conservation of Energy
KE + PE = constant
Total mechanical energy stays the same (ignoring friction). As PE decreases, KE increases and vice versa.
Example: Ball at top: all PE. At bottom: all KE. Halfway: half and half.
Roller coaster: highest point = max PE, lowest point = max KE. Total never changes.

Fluids & Pressure
5 formulas ▼
Pressure
P = F / A
Pressure = Force per unit Area. Same force on smaller area = higher pressure (think stiletto vs sneaker).
Example: 100 N on 0.5 m²: P = 200 Pa
Pascal's Law
F₁/A₁ = F₂/A₂
Pressure is transmitted equally. Small piston pushes small force, big piston pushes big force. This is how hydraulics work.
Example: Small piston: 10 N on 2 cm². Large piston: 20 cm² → F = 100 N
Hydraulic brakes, car jacks — same principle. Small effort, big result.
Continuity Equation
A₁v₁ = A₂v₂
Flow rate is constant. Smaller pipe = faster flow. Thumb over a garden hose = faster water.
Example: Pipe narrows from 4 cm² to 1 cm²: velocity increases 4x
Bernoulli's Principle
Faster fluid = Lower pressure
As fluid speed increases, pressure decreases. This explains airplane lift, curveballs, and atomizers.
Airplane wings: air moves FASTER over top = LOWER pressure = lift!
Buoyancy
Float if object density < fluid density
An object floats if it's less dense than the fluid. Ice floats on water. Steel sinks. Ships float because of hull shape (average density < water).

Electricity
4 formulas ▼
Ohm's Law
V = I × R   |   I = V/R   |   R = V/I
V = Voltage (Volts), I = Current (Amps), R = Resistance (Ohms). Cover the variable you want.
VIR Triangle — Cover what you need
Example: V = 12V, R = 4Ω: I = 12/4 = 3 Amps
VIR triangle: V on top, I × R on bottom. Cover what you need, the rest is the formula.
Electrical Power
P = VI = I²R = V²/R
Power in Watts. Three forms — use whichever matches the variables you have.
Example: 120V, 2A: P = 120 × 2 = 240 W
Series Circuits
Rtotal = R₁ + R₂ + R₃
Same current everywhere. Voltage divides across components. One break = entire circuit dies (Christmas lights).
Series Circuit
Series = Single path. One break kills everything. R adds up.
Parallel Circuits
1/R = 1/R₁ + 1/R₂
Same voltage across each branch. Current divides. Each branch is independent — one break doesn't kill others.
Parallel Circuit
Example: R1=6Ω, R2=3Ω: 1/R = 1/6 + 1/3 = 3/6 → R = 2Ω
Parallel = multiple Paths. Each branch independent. Total R is LESS than smallest individual R.

Momentum & Impulse
5 formulas ▼
Momentum
p = mv
Momentum = mass × velocity. Units: kg·m/s. A vector quantity (has direction).
\( p = mv \)
Example: 2 kg at 5 m/s → p = 2 × 5 = 10 kg·m/s
Impulse
J = FΔt = mΔv
Impulse = force × time = change in momentum. Units: N·s (same as kg·m/s).
\( J = F\Delta t = m\Delta v \)
Example: 20 N for 3 s → J = 20 × 3 = 60 N·s
Impulse is the PUSH over TIME — bigger force or longer time = more impulse.
Conservation of Momentum
pbefore = pafter
In a closed system (no external forces), total momentum before = total momentum after.
\( m_1 v_1 + m_2 v_2 = m_1 v_1' + m_2 v_2' \)
Example: 3 kg at 4 m/s hits stationary 1 kg → 12 kg·m/s total before = total after
Elastic Collision
KE + p  both conserved
Both kinetic energy AND momentum are conserved. Objects bounce apart (e.g., billiard balls).
Key: KE before = KE after, AND p before = p after
Inelastic Collision
m₁v₁ = (m₁ + m₂)vf
Only momentum is conserved. Objects stick together. KE is lost (converted to heat/deformation).
\( m_1 v_1 = (m_1 + m_2) v_f \)
Example: 0.5 kg at 8 m/s sticks to 1.5 kg → v = 4/2 = 2 m/s

Kinematics
8 formulas ▼
Displacement
Δx = xf - xi
Displacement = final position − initial position. A vector (has direction).
\( \Delta x = x_f - x_i \)
Example: Start at 3 m, end at 11 m → Δx = 11 − 3 = 8 m
Average Velocity
v = Δx / Δt
Average velocity = displacement ÷ time. Units: m/s.
\( v = \frac{\Delta x}{\Delta t} \)
Example: 100 m in 20 s → v = 100/20 = 5 m/s
Acceleration
a = Δv / Δt
Acceleration = change in velocity ÷ time. Units: m/s².
\( a = \frac{\Delta v}{\Delta t} \)
Example: 0 to 20 m/s in 5 s → a = 20/5 = 4 m/s²
SUVAT: Final Velocity
vf = vi + at
Final velocity = initial velocity + (acceleration × time).
\( v_f = v_i + at \)
Example: Start at rest, a = 4 m/s², t = 5 s → v = 0 + 4×5 = 20 m/s
SUVAT: Displacement
Δx = vit + ½at²
Displacement with constant acceleration. Use when you don't know final velocity.
\( \Delta x = v_i t + \tfrac{1}{2}at^2 \)
Example: From rest, a = 2 m/s², t = 10 s → d = 0 + ½(2)(100) = 100 m
SUVAT: Velocity-Displacement
vf² = vi² + 2aΔx
Use when time is NOT given. Relates velocity, acceleration, and displacement.
\( v_f^2 = v_i^2 + 2a\Delta x \)
Example: 20 m/s brakes over 50 m → 0 = 400 + 100a → a = -4 m/s²
Projectile Components
v0x = v₀cosθ   |   v0y = v₀sinθ
Break initial velocity into horizontal and vertical components for projectile problems.
\( v_{0x} = v_0 \cos\theta \quad|\quad v_{0y} = v_0 \sin\theta \)
Example: 50 m/s at 30° → v0x = 50 cos30° = 43.3, v0y = 50 sin30° = 25
Centripetal Acceleration
ac = v² / r
Centripetal acceleration points toward the center. Faster speed or smaller radius = more acceleration.
\( a_c = \frac{v^2}{r} \)
Example: v = 10 m/s, r = 5 m → ac = 100/5 = 20 m/s²
SUVAT equations — know any 3 variables, find the other 2. No time? Use v² = v⊂i² + 2aΔx.

Gas Laws
4 formulas ▼
Ideal Gas Law
PV = nRT
P = pressure, V = volume, n = moles, R = gas constant (8.314 J/mol·K), T = temperature (Kelvin).
\( PV = nRT \)
Key: Temperature must be in Kelvin (K = °C + 273).
Boyle's Law (Constant T)
P₁V₁ = P₂V₂
At constant temperature, pressure and volume are inversely related. Squeeze → smaller volume, higher pressure.
\( P_1 V_1 = P_2 V_2 \)
Example: 5 L at 1 atm → 2 atm → V = 5×1/2 = 2.5 L
Boyle = Boys squeeze Balloons — more pressure = less volume.
Charles's Law (Constant P)
V₁/T₁ = V₂/T₂
At constant pressure, volume and temperature are directly related. Heat it → it expands.
\( \frac{V_1}{T_1} = \frac{V_2}{T_2} \)
Example: 6 L at 300 K → 600 K → V = 6×600/300 = 12 L
Combined Gas Law
P₁V₁/T₁ = P₂V₂/T₂
Use when both pressure AND temperature change. Combines Boyle's and Charles's Laws.
\( \frac{P_1 V_1}{T_1} = \frac{P_2 V_2}{T_2} \)
Key: If T is constant → Boyle's. If P is constant → Charles's.

Heat Transfer
5 formulas ▼
Conduction
q = kA(ΔT/Δx)
Heat through direct contact. k = thermal conductivity, A = area, ΔT = temp difference, Δx = thickness.
\( q = kA\frac{\Delta T}{\Delta x} \)
Example: Metal spoon in hot soup — heat conducts through the solid from hot to cold end.
Convection
q = hAΔT
Heat through fluid movement (liquid or gas). h = heat transfer coefficient. Warm fluid rises, cool sinks.
\( q = hA\Delta T \)
Example: Warm air rising from a heater, boiling water — fluid carries heat via circulation.
Radiation
q = eσAT⁴
Heat via electromagnetic waves. No medium needed — works through vacuum (how the Sun heats Earth).
\( q = e\sigma A T^4 \)
Example: Feeling warmth from a campfire without touching it — that's radiant heat.
Three Types Summary
Conduction  |  Convection  |  Radiation
Contact (solids) | Currents (fluids) | Rays (electromagnetic, no medium). All move heat from hot to cold.
OAR Tip: Conduction = spoon, Convection = boiling water, Radiation = campfire warmth at distance.
"CCR" — Contact, Currents, Rays. Three types, easy to remember.
Phase Diagram / Triple Point
Solid + Liquid + Gas coexist
The triple point is the unique temperature & pressure where all three phases exist together in equilibrium.
Key: Water's triple point: 0.01°C at 611.73 Pa — ice, water, and steam coexist.

Magnetism
5 formulas ▼
Magnetic Poles
Like → Repel   |   Opposite → Attract
N-N or S-S repel. N-S attract. Same rule as electric charges — like repels, opposite attracts.
OAR Tip: If two magnets push apart, they're showing the same pole.
Ferromagnetic Materials
Iron  |  Cobalt  |  Nickel
Only these three elements are naturally ferromagnetic — they can be permanently magnetized and strongly attracted to magnets.
Key: Copper, aluminum, gold, silver are NOT magnetic.
"ICN" — Iron, Cobalt, Nickel. The magnetic trio.
Electromagnets
Current + Coil + Iron Core = Electromagnet
Electricity flowing through a coil creates a magnetic field. Adding an iron core strengthens it dramatically.
Key: Electromagnets can be turned on/off — permanent magnets cannot.
Transformer Equation
V₁/V₂ = N₁/N₂
Voltage ratio equals turns ratio. More turns on secondary = step-up (higher voltage). Fewer = step-down.
\( \frac{V_1}{V_2} = \frac{N_1}{N_2} \)
Example: 120V input, 100 turns primary, 500 turns secondary → V₂ = 120 × 500/100 = 600V
Motors, Generators & Diodes
Motor: Electrical → Mechanical  |  Generator: Mechanical → Electrical
Motors convert electricity to motion. Generators do the reverse. Diodes allow current in one direction only. Capacitance: C = Q/V.
\( C = \frac{Q}{V} \)  (Capacitance in Farads)
OAR Tip: Motor = electricity powers movement. Generator = movement creates electricity.
